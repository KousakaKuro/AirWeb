Carbon Monoxide

What is Carbon Monoxide (CO)?

Carbon monoxide (CO) is an odorless, colorless and tasteless but dangerous gas. Carbon monoxide is produced when fuels such as gasoline, natural gas, oil, kerosene, wood or charcoal are burned. Breathing CO reduces the blood's ability to carry oxygen. It can reach dangerous levels indoors or outdoors.

What Are the Health Effects of Nitrogen Dioxide Pollution?

Once inhaled, CO attaches to the hemoglobin in the red blood cells. Hemoglobin normally carries oxygen throughout the body. When CO attaches to the hemoglobin, it blocks the oxygen the body must have, creating health problems.

Many of these symptoms are similar to the flu, food poisoning or other illnesses. So you may not suspect CO poisoning. If symptoms persist, and especially if the symptoms get better after you leave the building, CO may be the cause.

What Are the Sources of Sulfur Dioxide Emissions?

  • Gas appliances (furnaces, ranges, ovens, water heaters, clothes dryers, etc.)
  • Coal or oil furnaces
  • Fireplaces, wood stoves
  • Space heaters or oil or kerosene heaters
  • Gas-powered lawn mowers and power tools
  • Charcoal grills, camp stoves
  • Automobile exhaust fumes

How can you protect yourself from carbon monoxide?

  • Ensure all appliances are properly vented.
  • Install and operate appliances according to manufacturers' instructions and local codes.
  • Avoid using unvented appliances.
  • Regularly inspect and clean heating systems, chimneys, and flues.
  • Ensure furnaces have sufficient outside air intake.
  • Use appliances and stoves correctly; do not use ovens or gas ranges for heating.
  • Never burn charcoal, use kerosene lanterns, or portable camp stoves indoors.
  • Avoid operating gasoline-powered engines in enclosed areas like garages.
  • Do not leave cars or lawnmowers running in closed garages.
  • Keep portable generators far from the home and away from windows or doors.
  • Prohibit smoking inside the home, as tobacco products also emit carbon monoxide.
  • Install carbon monoxide detectors with audible alarms near sleeping areas, adjacent to garages, and close to combustion equipment.
  • Refrain from exercising near busy streets or highways.
